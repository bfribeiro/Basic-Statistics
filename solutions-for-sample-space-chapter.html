Exercise 1


# Let's build the sample space.

child_1<-c("M","F")
child_2<-c("M","F")
child_3<-c("M","F")

S<-expand.grid('Child 1'=child_1,'Child 2'=child_2,'Child 3'=child_3)

# I did this answer using only the expand.grid function so you can see another way of
# constructig the sample space. Since the sample space is small, you can simply count
# the possibilities.

S
##   Child 1 Child 2 Child 3
## 1       M       M       M
## 2       F       M       M
## 3       M       F       M
## 4       F       F       M
## 5       M       M       F
## 6       F       M       F
## 7       M       F       F
## 8       F       F       F


# TWO CHILDREN OF THE SAME SEX

# You can grep the subsets of S that satisfy your conditions using the grepl function
# For two males there the following combinations:

subset(S, grepl("M", S$`Child 1`) & grepl("M", S$`Child 2`) & grepl("F", S$`Child 3`))
##   Child 1 Child 2 Child 3
## 5       M       M       F
subset(S, grepl("M", S$`Child 1`) & grepl("F", S$`Child 2`) & grepl("M", S$`Child 3`))
##   Child 1 Child 2 Child 3
## 3       M       F       M
subset(S, grepl("F", S$`Child 1`) & grepl("M", S$`Child 2`) & grepl("M", S$`Child 3`))
##   Child 1 Child 2 Child 3
## 2       F       M       M

# For two females there the following combinations:

subset(S, grepl("F", S$`Child 1`) & grepl("F", S$`Child 2`) & grepl("M", S$`Child 3`))
##   Child 1 Child 2 Child 3
## 4       F       F       M
subset(S, grepl("F", S$`Child 1`) & grepl("M", S$`Child 2`) & grepl("F", S$`Child 3`))
##   Child 1 Child 2 Child 3
## 6       F       M       F
subset(S, grepl("M", S$`Child 1`) & grepl("F", S$`Child 2`) & grepl("F", S$`Child 3`))
##   Child 1 Child 2 Child 3
## 7       M       F       F

# As you can see, there are three possibilities for each, so there 6 possilities of two
# children have the same sex.

# If you prefer to use the function we created:

combine <- function(..., prefix = "", suffix='', sep = ", ") {
  paste0(prefix, levels(interaction(..., sep = sep)), suffix)
}

S<-matrix(combine('Child 1'=child_1,'Child 2'=child_2,'Child 3'=child_3), nrow=8, ncol=1)

# You can use the grep function, as we did in through the chapter
grep("M, M, F", S, value=T)
## [1] "M, M, F"
grep("M, F, M", S, value=T)
## [1] "M, F, M"
grep("M, F, M", S, value=T)
## [1] "M, F, M"

# Repeat the same for the female.

# Or, you can use the grepl function inside the sum function.
# grepl provides a logical result. True or False. True is equivalent to 1, so whe  you
# sum, it will count the True values and add them up.

sum(c(grepl("M, M, F", S), grepl("M, F, M", S), grepl("F, M, M", S)),
      grepl("F, F, M", S), grepl("F, M, F", S), grepl("M, F, F", S))
## [1] 6

# AT LEAST ONE MALE CHILD

# Here we can use the properties we learned. To have at least one male child means that 
# I can't have three female children. So you can use the complement function. If I
# can't have three female children, then everything that is not three female children
# satisfies the condition.

# We can use setdiff:

length(setdiff(S,'F, F, F'))
## [1] 7

# Or we can use grepl:

sum(!grepl("F, F, F", S)) 
## [1] 7
# New operator here. Using ! before grepl, I'm telling it that I want everything that is
# not "F, F, F".

# NO MORE THAN TWO FEMALE CHILDREN

# No more than two female means that everything that is not "F, F, F" is the answer. So it's
# exactly the same answer that AT LEAST ONE MALE CHILD.

There is no calculation in this exercise. Itâ€™s all theory answers:

  • only \(A\) happens: \(A \cap B^{c} \cap C^{c}\)
  • \(A\) and \(C\) happens but not \(B\): \(A \cap C \cap B^{c}\)
  • All events happen: \(A \cap B \cap C\)
  • At least one event happens: \(A \cup B \cup C\)
  • No event happens:\(A^{c} \cap B^{c} \cap C^{c}\)
  • Exactly one event happen:\((A \cap B^{c} \cap C^{c}) \cup (A^{c} \cap B \cap C^{c}) \cup (A^{c} \cap B^{c} \cap C)\)
  • No more than two events happen: \((A \cap B \cap C)^{c}\)
